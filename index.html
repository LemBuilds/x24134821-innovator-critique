The Crisis

The data gap
is a justice gap.

Ghana's wildlife is documented mostly by outsiders. The communities who live with it every day — who know its patterns, its seasons, its secrets — have been cut out of the picture entirely.

Fraction
Of sightings come from local communities[6]
Community-contributed observations represent a small fraction of formal biodiversity datasets in West African protected areas — despite residents being present 365 days a year.
Fastest Growing
Africa safari tourism segment[2]
Safari tourism is Africa's fastest-growing travel segment (Grand View Research, 2033 outlook). Professional photographers — predominantly non-local — capture almost all of this value. Community members receive nothing.
$4/day
Average household income in buffer zone communities[5]
Subsistence farmers and guides living adjacent to Ghana's northern protected areas earn below the international poverty threshold — while the land around them generates millions for others.
Severely
Underdoc.
West Africa's wildlife coverage gap[3]
West Africa's wildlife is significantly underdocumented — conservation capacity lags East Africa by an estimated order of magnitude (IUCN, BWF, academic consensus). No platform exists for Dagbanli-speaking contributors.
"I have seen more lions than any researcher who has ever come to Larabanga. But I have never been asked. Nobody gave me a way to say what I know."
Ibrahim D., subsistence farmer & informal wildlife guide, Larabanga — Mole National Park buffer zone
