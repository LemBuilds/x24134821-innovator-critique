# 03 — Problem Statement

## The Real Problem

Ghana's 16 protected areas cover 5.2% of national territory. Yet over **78% of all biodiversity monitoring data** is generated by a small cohort of professional researchers and NGO-affiliated observers — most of whom are non-resident.

The **340,000+ residents** living within 20km of Ghana's northern savanna protected areas possess daily, high-resolution observational knowledge of wildlife movements, crop-raiding incidents, and habitat shifts that goes entirely unrecorded. This data vacuum produces three cascading failures:

---

### Failure 1 — The Conservation Data Gap
Conservation managers at Mole National Park make decisions on incomplete spatial and temporal data. Less than **8% of wildlife observations in West African protected areas** are submitted by local community members, creating blind spots in seasonal migration mapping, population trend analysis, and conflict hotspot identification.

### Failure 2 — Economic Exclusion
Professional wildlife photographers — predominantly non-local, often non-African — capture and license high-value imagery from Ghana's protected areas. The African wildlife photography market is worth **$2.3 billion annually**. Community members receive no share of this value, despite providing the local knowledge, access guidance, and habitat stewardship that makes those images possible.

### Failure 3 — Anti-Conservation Feedback Loop
Human-wildlife conflict goes undocumented and unmitigated. Without economic benefit or agency in the conservation system, communities develop adversarial relationships with protected areas, driving illegal grazing, retaliatory killing of predators, and political resistance to buffer zone expansion.

---

## Narrow User Segment

**Smallholder farmers and community guides aged 18–45, living within 20km of Mole National Park (Damongo District, Savannah Region, Ghana), who:**
- Own or share access to a smartphone (Android, entry-level)
- Have daily or weekly exposure to wildlife through farming, grazing, or informal guiding
- Lack structured tools to monetise or formally contribute their wildlife knowledge
- Earn below $4/day household income, making micro-income streams materially significant

**Estimated addressable population:** ~18,000 individuals in the primary buffer zone (Damongo, Larabanga, Murugu, and surrounding communities).

---

## The Opportunity

The same gap that produces failure is also a market: **verified, time-stamped, geo-tagged wildlife photography from community contributors** has immediate buyers — conservation NGOs, academic researchers, ecotourism operators, media publishers, and government biodiversity reporting bodies. The infrastructure to close this gap does not yet exist at scale in West Africa.
