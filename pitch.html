01 The best problems hide inside good stories. Starting with a short story — a lion, a pear, moonlight — forced specificity that a dry problem statement never would. Narrative is a research tool.
02 Narrow your user segment until it feels uncomfortably specific. "Communities near parks" is too broad. "Dagbanli-speaking smallholder farmers within 20km of Mole, aged 18–45" is a real person you can design for.
03 The exclusion from a market is often the market. The wildlife photography economy excluded community contributors for decades — creating an untapped supply gap that is simultaneously a justice issue and a business opportunity.
04 Offline-first is not a compromise — it's a competitive moat. Building for the most constrained user produces solutions that work for everyone. Most tech companies build for the least constrained user and call it inclusive later.
05 Cooperative economics changes the incentive structure entirely. The 20% cooperative fund makes SavannaLens a community asset, not a foreign extraction tool — which is the difference between adoption and rejection in these markets.
06 Precision over confidence: statistics must be sourced. Early versions cited fabricated statistics. Correcting them with real data — smaller numbers, less dramatic but honest — makes the pitch stronger, not weaker.
07 The most important parameter was the one nobody chose. Moonlight — the surprise 5th element — made the story emotionally true. The best constraints are the ones you didn't know you needed.
