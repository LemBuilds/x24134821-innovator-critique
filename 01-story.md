The Harmattan had blown itself out by the time Kofi Asante reached the edge of the watering hole, his boots red with laterite dust and his camera bag heavier than his patience. He had been tracking the old male lion for three days through Mole National Park, following rumours from the Larabanga villagers who spoke of a beast with a torn ear that appeared only by moonlight near a wild pear grove at the northern boundary. On the fourth night, under a full moon so bright it threw hard shadows across the clay earth, Kofi found him: the lion, slow and regal, lifting his great scarred head to nose at a pale pear that had fallen from the overhanging tree, its skin split with sweetness and alive with the scent of fermentation. Kofi steadied his breath, pressed the shutter, and caught the precise instant the lion's amber eye found the lens — a gaze so direct it felt less like a photograph and more like a contract, some ancient agreement between witness and the witnessed, sealed in silver light above the Ghanaian savanna — and as he walked back to the village that night he knew with certainty that no one had ever recorded this moment, because no one in Larabanga had ever been given the tools to try.
